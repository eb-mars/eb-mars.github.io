Landscape Evolution Modeling of Cratered Surfaces (like Mars) - ongoing

In an ongoing project, we are using Landscape Evolution Modeling to explore the different influences of cratered landscapes and climate on river networks, as an analog for Mars' past. This project utilises code that simulates crater topography (after Howward, 2007), and the stream power erosion algorithm from the open-source modeling framework LandLab. Preliminary results indicate that an existing crater topography has a significant effect on the structure and connectivity of rivers in the landscape, similar in significane to the effects of climate parameters.

Bamber, E. R., Goudge, T. A., & Stucky de Quay, G. (2024). How Do Impact Craters Influence Fluvial Structure? A Modeling Approach. 55th Lunar and Planetary Science Conference, 3040, 1110. link to conference abstract

Lonar Crater, Maharashtra, India: One of Earth’s few impact craters in basaltic rock.

Lonar Crater, Maharashtra, India.

At Lonar Crater, one of Earth’s few impact craters in basaltic rock (a great analog to Mars’ surface materials), there is an inlet valley. With local collaborators, and using cosmogenic Helium-3 for exposure age dating, we are investigating the timescale and rates of erosion of inlet formation at Lonar Crater.

Bamber, E., Goudge, T., Stucky de Quay, G., & Chandran, S. (2023). Investigating Crater Inlet Valley Formation: Field Study at Lonar Crater, India. EGU General Assembly Conference Abstracts, EGU-1437. link to conference abstract

Meteor Crater Boulder Rolling Experiment

During the 2022 Meteor Crater Field Camp, I was part of a team of graduate students who documented the roll of a boulder from the crater rim. Scientists have previously used boulder tracks on Mars and the Moon to understand the ‘softness’ of the surface, so the aim was to test those theories, where we could also measure the surface properties.

Kring, D. A., Bamber, E., Balance, A., Bretzfelder, J., et al. (2023). Cascading Boulder and Boulder Track Experiment at Barringer Meteorite Crater (aka Meteor Crater), Arizona. 54th Lunar and Planetary Science Conference, 2186. link to conference abstract

There are over 400 craters on Mars which have an inlet valley, which once supplied water to the crater interior (Left, a). However, there are thousands of craters which don’t have inlet valleys (Right, b). Why did some craters develop inlet valleys while some did not, despite other similarities?

What controls where inlet valleys form and impact craters become breached?

After investigating the mechanisms of crater-inlet valley formation on Mars, we got thinking about the thousands of craters on Mars which are not breached by an inlet valley. Why are they not breached, despite other similarities to the craters with inlet valleys? Using satellite data again, we investigated this question.

Bamber, E. R., Goudge, T. A., Fassett, C. I., Osinski, G. R., & Stucky de Quay, G. (2022). Paleolake Inlet Valley Formation: Factors Controlling Which Craters Breached on Early Mars. Geophysical Research Letters, 49(24). https://doi.org/10.1029/2022GL101097

How do valleys cross impact crater rim drainage divides?

The rims of fresh craters can be hundreds of meters high. Yet, on Earth, Mars and Titan, valleys have crossed into many craters forming inlet valleys. Using satellite data, we investigated craters on Mars to find out how crater-inlet valleys formed.

Bamber, E. R., Goudge, T. A., Fassett, C. I., & Osinski, G. R. (2022). Constraining the formation of paleolake inlet valleys across crater rims. Icarus, 378, 114945. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.icarus.2022.114945

Valleys on Mars Grace Fanson, 2025

MIT PhD Student

Grace is investigating the relationship between erosional depth in valleys and cumulative water volume, with applications to Mars science. During a visit to Cambridge, we will collaborate on simulating rainfall-driven erosion in basaltic Earth landscapes and synthetic landscapes, using the open-source modeling framework LandLab. In models, we can more easily track cumulative erosion (compared to in climate data), and so we can more robustly compare eroded depths with cumulative water supply. We can also test how different magnitudes and spatial distributions of rainfall affect total eroded depths, to fully qwuantify the relationship.

Ranfall on Mars, Ze-Wen Koh, 2025

MIT PhD Student

Ze-Wen's research centers on how precipitation is translated to erosion in river valleys, with application to Mars science. During a visit to Cambridge, we will collaborate on landscape evolution modeling in LandLab as a control experiment to compare to measurments made from natural landscapes. The plan is to vary precipitation and lithology in model runs of river erosion, then from the eroded landscape, calculate the modeled discharge. We will assess the relationship between precipitation-sourced discharge and fluvial erosion, and how precipitation patterns are reflected in the geomorphic record.

Hargraves Crater lake levels, Korbin Lawson, 2021

UT Austin external undergraduate research trainee experience (RTX)

Hargraves crater, a young fresh crater 120 km upstream of Jezero Crater (the Mars 2020 exploration site), has two clear sedimentary fan features. The smaller of these fans has been identified with great confidence as a delta by it's stratigraphic slope break at -800 m (Tebolt & Goudge, 2021), confirming late-stage lake activity on Mars! Korbin's goal was to determine if the other fan feature appeared similar in morphology, and/or had a similar topographic slope break (apex point, scarp front), which would support formation at a similar time, at the same lake level. The smaller delta apex occurs at -1128 m and the second, larger fan apex is at -1188 m. The apex elevation similarity supports an interpretation where the fans formed at the same time, in the same lake about 800m deep.

Lawson, K., Bamber, E. R. & Tebolt, M. Investigating the fluvio-lacustrine history of Hargraves crater, Mars. in AGU Fall Meeting (AGU, 2021). EP55A-1111. link to conference abstract

Inlet Concordance, Laura Arango, 2020

UT Austin GeoVISION external undergraduate summer research program

The source of water that carved Mars' valleys is hotly debated. Valleys carved by runoff (e.g., from rainfall), should follow local topographic slope, whereas valleys carved by other sources of water (e.g., meltwater beneath glaciers), would be less likely to follow topographic slope. Laura set out to measure how well the direction of crater-inlet valleys match with the direction of regional topographic slope, to begin exploring the possible sources of water for these features. Preliminary results illustrate that valleys into closed basin crater-lakes (CBLs) are less concordant with slope, compared to valleys into open basin crater-lakes (CBLs).

Histograms showing the mismatch in degrees between inlet valley direction and topographic slope direction [Upper panel] for closed basin crater-lakes (CBLs, orange) and open basin crater-lakes (OBLs, blue), and; [Lower panel] for valley network era valleys (VNs), and later isolated inlet valleys (IIs).
